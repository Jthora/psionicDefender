# Reference Checklist

## How to use this list
Treat these as verification anchors. Each item should be checked for:
- What is explicitly demonstrated.
- What is inferred but not demonstrated.
- Reproducibility constraints.

## High-priority references to verify
1. National Academies 2020 report on anomalous health incidents.
- Use for language accuracy: "directed pulsed RF appears plausible" for some cases.
- Keep caveat: multiple mechanisms were not ruled out.

2. Frey effect primary literature (microwave auditory effect).
- Confirms induced auditory perception under specific pulsed RF conditions.
- Does not by itself prove broad remote speech/control capability.

3. RF hearing effect patents.
- US 6,470,214 and US 6,587,729.
- Confirm patent claims and signal processing concepts.
- Do not treat patent grant as field-deployed proof.

4. Neurostimulation baseline.
- TMS/tDCS/tACS clinical and safety literature.
- Good for understanding what coupling levels and proximity are usually needed.

5. Exposure standards.
- RF: ICNIRP/IEEE.
- Airborne ultrasound: ICNIRP and national guidance.
- Low-frequency/infrasound occupational literature.

## Notes on weaker evidence classes
- Historical psychotronics literature can be useful for hypothesis generation but should be marked low-confidence unless independently replicated.
- Anecdotal incident reports should not be treated as mechanism proof without instrumented exposure correlation.

## Evidence grading template
For each claim, record:
- Claim text
- Source URL/DOI/patent id
- Evidence type: peer-reviewed / governmental report / patent / anecdote
- Reproducibility: high / medium / low / unknown
- Confidence: high / medium / low
- Open questions
